Assistant for Demonstrating Scientific Tools

Nowadays, there are numerous tools that assist us on a daily basis. Whether it's GPS navigation, task management, or communication tools, there are many solutions that make users' lives easier. At the heart of these are personal assistants. These assistants enable various tasks to be performed, bring together a range of existing tools, and make their use easier, for example, via voice commands. They also allow for obtaining information (such as weather updates, daily schedules, etc.), managing personal information, and interacting naturally with computers or other electronic devices. The emergence of these technologies was a revolution in the use of digital tools, as seen in home automation, for example, with assistants like Google Home and Alexa. However, these assistants have certain limitations. Despite advances in Text-to-Speech (TTS) technologies, the feeling of interacting with a machine can still be present. Moreover, the possibilities are quite limited when interacting solely with a voice; there is no display, which restricts the possible interactions. By expanding the capabilities of a virtual assistant, endowing it with abilities such as vision via camera, hearing, and speech, new opportunities arise, particularly in the field of research. In the context of research, specifically in computer science, in areas like computer vision, it would be highly beneficial to have an easy and playful way to deploy research projects. Providing a modular assistant to which new features could be easily added could facilitate the deployment of research activities. The central issue that emerges is thus the following: How to design and implement an interactive, modular virtual assistant capable of embracing these various abilities to facilitate playful experimentation in the context of research activities?
